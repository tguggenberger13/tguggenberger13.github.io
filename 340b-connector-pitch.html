01 — The Core Thesis

A Narrow Problem, Universally Shared,
With No Vendor Bothering to Solve It

The 340B Drug Pricing Program gives federally qualified health centers, rural health clinics, and qualifying hospitals access to deeply discounted pharmaceuticals — often 25–50% below wholesale acquisition cost. It is one of the most financially significant programs available to safety-net providers. And the reconciliation between the pharmacy data and the general ledger is, at virtually every covered entity in the country, done entirely by hand.

The data lives in a TPA like Macro Helix, Aventi, or CaptureRx. The GL lives in Sage Intacct or NetSuite. No one has built the connector. A CPA firm that builds it doesn't just sell a product — it walks into every FQHC relationship in its book with a solution to a problem that every CFO and Controller feels in their bones every single month.

~1,400

FQHCs in the U.S.

Every single one participates in 340B. Every single one is doing this reconciliation manually or not doing it accurately. This is not a market you have to convince — it's a market you have to reach.

01

The Rebate Shift Makes It Urgent Now

Manufacturers are moving from upfront 340B discounts to post-adjudicated rebate models. That transition turns a manageable spreadsheet problem into an active receivables management, accrual, and revenue recognition problem — one that ERPs are not configured to handle.

02

No Major Vendor Has Touched It

The niche is narrow enough that Workday, Oracle, and Sage themselves haven't built native connectors. The TPAs publish export formats but don't provide GL integration. This is a greenfield product opportunity with a captive, motivated buyer base.

The rebate model changes everything. In the traditional discount model, 340B savings hit immediately and bookkeeping is straightforward — you bought drugs cheap, you record the savings. In the emerging manufacturer rebate pilot model, covered entities must track receivables, manage accruals, and recognize revenue on a timeline they can't fully predict. Each rebate claim arrives as one of: approved, denied, pending, reversed, or tied up in a Good-Faith Inquiry. Their ERPs have none of this logic. The connector doesn't just automate what was manual — it implements financial logic that simply didn't exist before.

03 — Integration Architecture

Three Source Systems, One Connector

The connector has three realistic integration sources, each providing a different layer of the reconciliation picture. A complete implementation draws from all three — TPA for eligibility and savings detail, wholesaler for purchase confirmation, and 340B ESP for manufacturer compliance verification.

TPA Data Layer

Primary Source
Macro Helix · Aventi · CaptureRx · CarepathRx

The richest source. Contains actual 340B-qualified claim detail: drug, NDC, quantity, WAC price, 340B price, spread/savings. This is the data you need to post accurate GL entries and manage accruals under the rebate model.

Most TPAs provide structured CSV or EDI exports; several have API access available under data sharing agreements.

CSV / EDI / API

Wholesaler Invoices

Reconciliation Layer
Cardinal Health · McKesson · AmerisourceBergen

Cleaner and more structured than TPA data. Tells you what was purchased at 340B pricing — useful as a reconciliation layer against TPA eligibility data, particularly where manufacturer restrictions cause eligible vs. purchased to diverge.

All three major wholesalers offer purchase order and invoice data feeds via EDI 810 or portal export.

EDI 810 / Portal Export

340B ESP Claims

Compliance Layer
340B ESP (Manufacturer Compliance)

Increasingly required for covered entities as manufacturers use ESP to identify drugs subject to both PBM and 340B discounts. Produces structured claims data that feeds a manufacturer compliance reconciliation workflow — critical for entities under scrutiny.

Structured claims data available via the ESP portal for enrolled covered entities.

Structured Claims Export

Target GL destinations in v1.0: Sage Intacct and NetSuite — the two ERPs with dominant share in the FQHC / nonprofit healthcare market. Both have mature API ecosystems and well-documented journal entry endpoints. A Sage Intacct native app marketplace listing is achievable and would be a material distribution accelerant.

08 — Competitive Landscape

A Niche Too Small for Big Vendors,
Too Specialized for Generic Integrators

The competitive landscape for a 340B-to-GL connector is essentially empty. The few adjacent players either operate upstream (TPAs who stop at claims data), focus on a different problem (340B software platforms), or lack the ERP and accounting credibility to touch the GL. This is the definition of a white space.

ERP Vendors — Sage, Oracle NetSuite

The Platform Providers

Own the destination GL. Have massive development resources. Could build this natively if they chose to.

Weakness: The FQHC market is too small and too specialized for their product roadmaps. Neither Sage nor NetSuite has built native 340B support. This niche doesn't move their needle — which is precisely why the gap exists and persists.
TPAs — Macro Helix, Aventi, CaptureRx

The Data Owners

Sit on the richest source data. Understand the 340B program deeply. Have existing relationships with covered entities.

Weakness: TPAs are pharmacy operations companies, not finance technology companies. Their product focus is eligibility determination and contract pharmacy management — not GL integration. Building a GL connector would require ERP API expertise and accounting domain knowledge they don't have.
Generic iPaaS Tools — Workato, Boomi, MuleSoft

The Integrators

Could technically build point-to-point connectors between TPA exports and Intacct/NetSuite with enough configuration effort.

Weakness: No domain knowledge of 340B accounting rules, rebate model complexity, or FQHC chart of accounts conventions. A generic integration misses all the financial logic — duplicate discount detection, rebate accrual state management, exception handling — that makes the connector actually useful. Finance teams can't buy a plumbing tool; they need a solution.
340B Software Platforms — Verity, Sentry

The Compliance Tools

Purpose-built 340B compliance and analytics platforms. Some overlap in data visualization and reporting.

Weakness: Focus is on program compliance and audit defense, not GL posting. These tools stop at dashboards and reports — they don't write journal entries or manage ERP integration. They are a complement to the connector, not a substitute.

10 — Rollout Timeline

Build Narrow, Then Expand

The recommended approach is to build an extremely focused v1.0 — one TPA (Macro Helix, the largest), one GL (Sage Intacct, dominant in FQHCs), discount model only — and put it in front of 2–3 existing clients before building anything else. The connectors that fail do so because they try to support every TPA and every ERP before they've validated the core workflow. Build narrow, validate fast, then expand.

11 — The Verdict

A Product the Market Is Waiting For,
From a Firm It Will Actually Trust

What makes the 340B Reconciliation Connector exceptional as a CPA firm product initiative is the combination of factors that almost never align: a universally felt pain, a captive and enumerable market, no credible incumbent, a regulatory tailwind that's making the pain acuter, and a distribution advantage — existing client trust — that a startup simply cannot replicate. The investment is the most modest of the three practice opportunities presented in this series, the product is the most focused, and the go-to-market is the most direct. This is a product worth building.
