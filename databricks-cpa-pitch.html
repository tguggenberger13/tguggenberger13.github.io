01 — The Core Thesis

A Structural Gap the Big Four
Are Too Large to Fill

The Databricks consulting market is large and growing, yet concentrated at two ends: massive global SIs (Deloitte, Accenture) serving enterprise accounts, and Databricks' own professional services team, which is product-focused by design. The mid-market — companies with $200M–$2B in revenue — is systematically underserved. A top-20 CPA firm already has the client relationships, industry trust, and compliance expertise to own this space.

02 — Market Context

Why Now, Why Databricks

Databricks has become the dominant platform for unified data analytics, machine learning, and AI workloads. Its 2024 revenue exceeded $1.6B with a $62B private valuation. The platform is increasingly the default choice for data modernization — which means migrations and advisory engagements are a structural, multi-year opportunity, not a trend.

06 — Competitive Landscape

Who You'd Be Competing Against

The good news: none of the natural competitors are a perfect fit for mid-market, compliance-heavy clients. Each has a structural weakness that a CPA firm can exploit.

Tier 1 SIs — Deloitte, Accenture, EY

The Giants

Deep Databricks expertise, global delivery, strong brand. Will win Fortune 500 accounts comfortably.

Weakness: Minimum engagement sizes ($500K+) and overhead rates make mid-market engagements uneconomical. They won't hunt for $150K projects.
Databricks Internal PS Team

The Vendor

Deepest platform knowledge available. Preferred by some clients who want to go direct. Strong for narrow, product-specific acceleration.

Weakness: Scope is limited to Databricks-specific work. They won't touch compliance frameworks, ERP integration, or organizational change management.
Boutique Data Consultancies

The Specialists

Nimble, technically sharp, often ex-FAANG or ex-Big Tech. Strong delivery capability, lower overhead.

Weakness: No audit credibility, no compliance lineage, no existing client trust. Starting every engagement at zero on the relationship.
Your Firm's Position

The Opportunity Gap

Mid-market clients want a trusted, compliance-aware partner who can handle migration complexity and integrate with their existing audit/advisory relationship. No current competitor checks all of those boxes.

Advantage: You walk in with C-suite trust already established. The sales cycle is dramatically shorter when the CFO already returns your calls.

08 — Rollout Timeline

A Deliberate, Low-Risk Build

The recommended approach is a phased rollout that tests the thesis with minimal exposure before committing to full practice build-out. Each phase has clear success criteria before advancing.

Q1–Q2
Months 1–6 — Foundation

Hire, Partner, Validate

Hire the Practice Lead and one Senior Engineer. Enroll in the Databricks Partner Program. Identify 2–3 existing audit clients who are strong candidates for a pilot readiness assessment (discounted or internal-rate). Run one or two lightweight assessments to stress-test delivery capability and refine positioning.

Partner Status Achieved 2 Pilot Assessments Complete Team of 3 Onboarded
Q3–Q4
Months 7–12 — First Revenue

Close First Paid Engagements

Convert pilot clients to paid implementations. Hire second engineer. Begin attending Databricks Data + AI Summit and regional partner events. Target 4–6 paid engagements by year-end. Develop first case study from a successful pilot. Begin co-sell conversations with Databricks AE team.

Target Databricks Select or Premier partner tier. Begin converting implementations to retainer relationships. Gross revenue target: $2–3M.

09 — The Verdict

This Is a Practice Worth Building

The combination of existing client trust, compliance expertise, and a genuinely underserved mid-market creates a durable competitive position. The investment is modest, the revenue trajectory is credible, and the cross-sell flywheel is real. The firms that move early will lock in the partner relationships and case study credibility that make this compounding over time.
