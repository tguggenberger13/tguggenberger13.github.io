01 — The Core Thesis

Regulatory Mandate Meets
Proprietary Data Asset

Federal price transparency rules — CMS Hospital Price Transparency (effective 2021) and the Transparency in Coverage rule for payers (effective 2022) — have generated the largest forced disclosure of healthcare pricing data in history. The problem is that the data, as published, is nearly unusable: thousands of inconsistent Machine Readable Files, mismatched schemas, missing fields, and files that routinely exceed 10GB each.

A CPA firm that builds the infrastructure to ingest, normalize, and maintain this data at scale creates a proprietary competitive asset that no competitor can replicate quickly. That asset then powers four distinct advisory franchises — provider, payer, employer, and M&A — each billable at premium rates, each generating cross-sell into existing service lines.

The Moat

Data That Takes Years to Build

Ingesting and normalizing MRF files is a multi-year infrastructure effort. A firm that starts today builds a lead that is nearly impossible to close. The data asset appreciates over time as coverage, quality, and history deepen.

01

Regulatory Tailwind

CMS enforcement is accelerating. Civil Monetary Penalties now reach $300/day per hospital bed for non-compliance. Clients in all four segments face growing urgency — creating inbound demand rather than outbound selling.

02

No Obvious Owner

No advisory firm has credibly combined price transparency data expertise with audit credibility and healthcare compliance depth. This space is genuinely unowned — and a CPA firm is the most natural occupant.

02 — The Proprietary Platform

What the Firm Builds and Owns

Unlike the Databricks practice where the firm delivers expertise on someone else's platform, this practice is anchored by a proprietary data asset the firm owns outright. The platform has two primary data pillars — hospital and payer — each requiring distinct ingestion and normalization engineering.

Pillar 1 — Hospital Price Transparency (CDM / MRF)
Ingest CMS-mandated MRF files from 6,000+ U.S. hospitals
Normalize across schemas: Standard Charges, Payer-Negotiated Rates, De-Identified Min/Max
Map to standard code sets: CPT, HCPCS, DRG, MS-DRG, NDC
Track compliance posture per hospital (file presence, freshness, completeness)
Historical versioning to track rate changes over time
Facility metadata enrichment (system affiliation, bed count, ownership type)
Pillar 2 — Payer Price Transparency (TiC / MRF)
Ingest In-Network Rate files from major commercial payers (BCBS, Aetna, Cigna, UHC, etc.)
Normalize negotiated rate tables across NPI, billing code, and plan type
Ingest Out-of-Network Allowed Amount files
Reconcile provider NPIs to facility and practitioner master records
Map plan-level data to employer sponsor identifiers (TPA linkage)
Build cross-payer rate benchmarking by market, specialty, and code

The combined platform produces queryable, benchmarkable pricing intelligence across both supply (hospitals) and demand (payers) sides of the market — a view of healthcare economics that no client can construct on their own and no competitor currently offers in the advisory context.

03 — Market Context

Why the Urgency Is Real and Growing

Price transparency is no longer an emerging topic — it is an active enforcement environment. CMS has issued hundreds of warning letters and Civil Monetary Penalties. Payers face parallel pressure from the Consolidated Appropriations Act and state-level surprise billing laws. Clients across all four segments are actively looking for guidance, and few advisors have the data infrastructure to provide it credibly.

07 — Competitive Landscape

A Space With No Clear Owner

Price transparency advisory is early enough that no competitor has established a dominant position. The field is fragmented between data vendors, niche consultancies, and law firms — none of whom combine advisory credibility with data ownership and healthcare audit depth.

Big Four Consulting Arms

Deloitte, PwC, EY, KPMG

Healthcare practice depth and brand credibility. Will pursue large IDN and national payer accounts.

Weakness: No firm has built a dedicated price transparency data platform. Their engagements rely on client data or one-off scrapes — no proprietary benchmarking capability. Mid-market is underserved as always.
Data Vendors (RAND, Turquoise Health, etc.)

Price Transparency Data Companies

Strong on data collection and normalization. Some offer benchmarking tools and dashboards directly to clients.

Weakness: Pure data vendors, not advisors. They lack audit credibility, compliance expertise, and the trusted C-suite relationships to convert data into strategic action. Clients often use them as inputs, then still need advisory help.
Healthcare Boutiques & Law Firms

Specialized Consultancies

Deep regulatory expertise in specific segments (e.g., hospital compliance or benefits law). Trusted in their lanes.

Weakness: No cross-segment capability. A hospital compliance boutique can't serve payers and employers with the same platform. No proprietary data assets. Fragmented and narrow.
Benefits Consultants (Mercer, Aon, WTW)

Large Benefits Advisors

Deep employer relationships and plan design expertise. Increasingly aware of CAA and fiduciary risk for self-funded employers.

Weakness: Employer-focused only — cannot serve providers or payers. No audit credibility. Price transparency work is adjacent to their core business, not central to it. Conflict risk with carrier relationships.
